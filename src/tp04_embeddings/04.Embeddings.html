Where we are

Sessions 1–3 gave us a solid classical pipeline. Today we break its fundamental assumption.

The pipeline so far

Sessions 01–02
Raw text → tokens → vocabulary.
Normalisation, BPE, n-gram LMs.
Majority-class baseline: ~25% F1.
Session 03
Tokens → TF-IDF vector → label.
Naive Bayes, Logistic Regression.
NB ~83% · LR ~89% macro F1.
Today — Session 04
Tokens → dense vector → label.
Word embeddings. Mean pooling.
Goal: handle semantics, not just counts.
Key question today: TF-IDF gives every word its own independent dimension. Can we do better — can we encode meaning into the vector itself?

The problem with TF-IDF

Problem 1 — Synonymy
"The car crashed." vs "The automobile crashed."

TF-IDF treats car and automobile as completely unrelated dimensions. A model trained on "car" has no idea "automobile" means the same thing.
Problem 2 — Sparsity
AG News vocabulary: ~50,000 words.
Each document: maybe 50 words.
Vector: 49,950 zeros out of 50,000 dimensions.

Sparse vectors are hard to generalise from.
Problem 3 — No geometry of meaning
In TF-IDF space:
distance("king", "queen") = distance("king", "pizza")

There is no sense in which "king" and "queen" are near each other. The representation has no semantic structure.
Problem 4 — OOV
New word at test time? It gets a zero vector.
The model has never seen it, has nothing to say about it.

Especially bad for rare words, names, technical terms.

What we want instead

Ideal property: words with similar meanings should have similar vectors. The geometry of the space should reflect the semantics of the language.
Dense
Not 50,000 dimensions.
Typically 100–300 dimensions.
Every dimension carries information.
No wasted zeros.
Semantic geometry
Similar words → nearby vectors.
cos(king, queen) ≈ 0.85
cos(king, pizza) ≈ 0.04
Distance reflects meaning.
Reusable
Train once on a large corpus.
Use on any downstream task.
The original "pre-trained model".
The question is: how do we learn such vectors from raw text, with no labels, no human annotation?
01

The Distributional Hypothesis

"You shall know a word by the company it keeps." — Firth, 1957

The core intuition

"You shall know a word by the company it keeps."

J.R. Firth, 1957 — long before neural networks existed.

Example
You encounter the word tezgüino — you have never seen it before.

Context sentences:
"A bottle of tezgüino is on the table."
"Everyone enjoyed the tezgüino."
"We make tezgüino out of corn."

You don't know the word — but you know it's a drink.
Why this works
Words that appear in similar contexts tend to have similar meanings.

"dog" and "cat" both appear near: pet, feed, vet, fur.
"dog" and "democracy" do not share contexts.

Idea: represent a word by the distribution of its contexts. Words with similar distributions get similar vectors.

From context to vector

Concrete example. A small corpus about food and animals.

Sentences (corpus)
"The dog ate the food."
"The cat ate the food."
"The dog ran in the park."
"The cat ran in the park."
"The car ran out of fuel."
Co-occurrence counts (window=1)
word ate ran park fuel
dog 1 1 1 0
cat 1 1 1 0
car 0 1 0 1
Observation: "dog" and "cat" have identical co-occurrence vectors. "car" is different. The counts already encode that dogs and cats are similar, cars are not. Word2Vec and GloVe learn dense versions of exactly this intuition.
02

Word2Vec

Mikolov et al., 2013 — the paper that changed NLP.

The Word2Vec idea

Key insight: instead of building a co-occurrence matrix and factorising it, train a tiny neural network to predict context from a word. The learned weights are the embeddings.
Skip-gram
Given a centre word, predict its context words.

Sentence: "The cat sat on the mat"
Window = 2:
Input: cat
Targets: The, sat, on... wait, wrong order.

Input: sat
Targets: The, cat, on, the
CBOW (Continuous Bag of Words)
Given the context words, predict the centre word.

Input: The, cat, on, the
Target: sat

Faster to train. Skip-gram works better for rare words. 
# Conceptual skip-gram training loop
for sentence in corpus:
    for i, centre in enumerate(sentence):
        # collect context window
        context = sentence[
            max(0, i-window) : i
        ] + sentence[
            i+1 : i+window+1
        ]
        for target in context:
            # gradient step:
            # push embed(centre) closer
            # to embed(target)
            update(embed[centre],
                   embed[target])
No labels needed. The corpus is the supervision signal. This is self-supervised learning.

Skip-gram — worked example

Sentence: "The quick brown fox jumps over the lazy dog"   Window size: 2
Training pairs generated from "fox"
Centre word Context word
foxbrown
foxquick
foxjumps
foxover
With a vocabulary of 10,000 words and a corpus of 1 billion tokens, you generate ~5 billion training pairs. Training runs for hours on a GPU. Result: a 300-dimensional vector for every word in the vocabulary.
What the network learns
Input layer: one-hot vector for the centre word.
Hidden layer: 300 neurons — no activation.
Output layer: softmax over vocab.

The hidden layer weights are the embeddings.
After training, we throw away the output layer.

The network is just a means to an end. The weights are the product.

Negative sampling — why it matters

Problem: softmax over a 100,000-word vocabulary requires computing 100,000 dot products per gradient step. With billions of training pairs, this is impossibly slow.
The trick — negative sampling
Instead of a full softmax, turn it into a binary classification problem:

Given the pair (fox, jumps): is this a real context pair? → YES (positive)

Given the pair (fox, parliament): real context? → NO (negative, randomly sampled)

Train to output 1 for positives, 0 for negatives. Only update the embeddings of the ~5 negative samples, not all 100,000 words.
Effect on training
Each step: update 1 positive + ~5 negatives = 6 vectors.
vs. full softmax: update all 100,000 vectors.

Speedup: ~16,000×

Negative words are sampled proportionally to their frequency raised to the 3/4 power — this empirically gives better embeddings than uniform sampling.
This is the key engineering insight that made Word2Vec practical. The idea predates Word2Vec but was first applied at this scale.

CBOW — the other direction

Continuous Bag of Words (CBOW) is the mirror image of Skip-gram: given the context words, predict the centre word. Same weights, opposite data flow.
Skip-gram (recap)
Input: centre word
Output: each context word independently

Sentence: "the quick brown fox jumps"
Input: fox
Targets: quick, brown, jumps, over

One centre → many predictions.
Better for rare words.
CBOW
Input: all context words averaged
Output: centre word

Sentence: "the quick brown fox jumps"
Input: mean of [quick, brown, jumps, over]
Target: fox

Many inputs → one prediction.
Faster to train, better on frequent words.
Network architecture — CBOW
  1. One-hot encode each context word → shape (V,)
  2. Average the one-hot vectors → shape (V,)
  3. W₁: (V, d) — projects to embedding space
  4. Hidden layer: (d,) — no activation
  5. W₂: (d, V) — projects back to vocab
  6. Softmax → probability over vocabulary
The embedding for word w is row w of W₁ (or the average of W₁ row and W₂ column).
In the TP: you will implement CBOW with Keras on a small toy corpus. The hidden layer weights are the embeddings — you will extract and visualise them.

Skip-gram vs CBOW — side by side

Data flow
Skip-gram CBOW
Input Centre word Context words (averaged)
Target Each context word Centre word
Pairs per token 2 × window 1
Training speed Slower Faster
Rare words Better Weaker
Typical use Research, rare vocab Production, large corpora
Example — window = 1
Sentence: "the king is royal"

Skip-gram pairs:
(king, the) · (king, is)
(is, king) · (is, royal)

CBOW pairs:
input mean([the, is]) → target king
input mean([king, royal]) → target is
Bottom line: both algorithms learn the same distributional hypothesis. The embeddings they produce are qualitatively similar. CBOW is what you implement in the TP because it maps cleanly to a single Keras Dense layer.
03

GloVe

Pennington, Socher, Manning — Stanford, 2014.

GloVe — Global Vectors

Word2Vec problem: it trains on local context windows and never directly sees the global co-occurrence statistics of the corpus. GloVe fixes this.
GloVe objective
Build the full co-occurrence matrix X first. X[i][j] = how often word j appears in the context of word i across the whole corpus.

Then find vectors w_i and w_j such that:
w_i · w_j + b_i + b_j ≈ log X[i][j]
The dot product of two word vectors should approximate the log of their co-occurrence count.
Why log?
Co-occurrence counts span several orders of magnitude.
"the" co-occurs with everything: count ≈ 10,000,000.
"astronaut" co-occurs with "moon": count ≈ 200.

Log compresses this range and makes the objective stable.
Word2Vec vs GloVe in practice
Word2Vec GloVe
Statistics Local window Global matrix
Training SGD on pairs Least squares on matrix
Best for Syntax, rare words Analogies, semantics
In practice Performance is very similar. Use whichever is available.
For this course we use GloVe 6B (trained on Wikipedia + Gigaword, 6 billion tokens, 400K vocabulary, 100d or 300d vectors). Free to download.

The Geometry of Meaning

Cosine similarity · analogies · nearest neighbours

Cosine similarity

Why cosine, not Euclidean distance?
Embedding vectors vary in magnitude — long documents produce longer mean vectors, frequent words may have larger norms.

Cosine similarity ignores magnitude and measures only the angle between two vectors.

cos(u, v) = (u · v) / (||u|| · ||v||)
Range: −1 (opposite) to +1 (identical direction).
AG News examples (GloVe 100d)
cos(football, soccer) ≈ 0.91
cos(football, basketball) ≈ 0.85
cos(football, economy) ≈ 0.14
cos(football, politics) ≈ 0.21

The geometry reflects what we intuitively know about these concepts. 
import numpy as np

def cosine_similarity(u: np.ndarray,
                      v: np.ndarray) -> float:
    """
    Compute cosine similarity between two vectors.

    Parameters
    ----------
    u : np.ndarray, shape (d,)
    v : np.ndarray, shape (d,)

    Returns
    -------
    float
        Similarity in [-1, 1].
    """
    return np.dot(u, v) / (
        np.linalg.norm(u) * np.linalg.norm(v)
    )

# Example
emb = load_glove("glove.6B.100d.txt")
cos = cosine_similarity(
    emb["football"],
    emb["soccer"]
)
print(f"{cos:.3f}")  # → 0.914

Analogies — king − man + woman ≈ queen

The most famous result in Word2Vec: vector arithmetic in embedding space encodes semantic relationships.
How it works
The direction man → woman encodes the concept "change gender".
Apply that direction to king:

v(king) − v(man) + v(woman) ≈ v(queen)
Find the word in the vocabulary whose vector is closest to this result. Answer: queen.
More examples
Paris − France + Italy ≈ Rome (capitals)
walked − walk + run ≈ ran (verb tense)
biggest − big + tall ≈ tallest (superlative)
CEO − man + woman ≈ CFO ⚠ (bias!) 
def analogy(emb: dict,
            a: str,
            b: str,
            c: str,
            n: int = 5) -> list[str]:
    """
    Solve: a is to b as c is to ?.

    Parameters
    ----------
    emb : dict
        Word → embedding mapping.
    a, b, c : str
        Analogy words (a:b :: c:?).
    n : int
        Number of candidates to return.

    Returns
    -------
    list of str
        Top-n nearest neighbours to
        emb[b] - emb[a] + emb[c].
    """
    target = emb[b] - emb[a] + emb[c]
    exclude = {a, b, c}
    sims = {
        w: cosine_similarity(target, v)
        for w, v in emb.items()
        if w not in exclude
    }
    return sorted(sims,
                  key=sims.get,
                  reverse=True)[:n]

print(analogy(emb, "man", "king", "woman"))
# → ['queen', 'monarch', ...]

Using embeddings for classification

We have word vectors. But our classifier needs a document vector. The simplest approach: mean pooling.
Mean pooling
1. Tokenise the document.
2. Look up the embedding for each token.
3. Average all token vectors.
4. The mean vector is the document representation.

d_vec = (1/n) Σ embed(w_i)
Then feed d_vec into a logistic regression classifier.
Example
Document: "France wins the World Cup final"

Tokens: [france, wins, world, cup, final]
Look up 5 vectors in GloVe.
Average → one 100-dimensional vector.
Feed to LR → predict Sports. 
def mean_pool(
    tokens: list[str],
    embeddings: dict,
    dim: int = 100,
) -> np.ndarray:
    """
    Compute mean-pooled document embedding.

    Parameters
    ----------
    tokens : list of str
        Pre-tokenised document.
    embeddings : dict
        Word → np.ndarray mapping.
    dim : int
        Embedding dimension, used as
        fallback for OOV tokens.

    Returns
    -------
    np.ndarray, shape (dim,)
        Mean of token embeddings.
        Returns zero vector if no
        token has an embedding.
    """
    vecs = [
        embeddings[t]
        for t in tokens
        if t in embeddings
    ]
    if not vecs:
        return np.zeros(dim)
    return np.mean(vecs, axis=0)
05

Limits and What's Next

Why static embeddings are not the end of the story.

The polysemy problem

Fatal limitation of Word2Vec and GloVe: every word gets exactly one vector, regardless of how many meanings it has.
Polysemy — same word, different meanings
"I went to the bank to withdraw money."
→ financial institution

"She sat on the bank of the river."
→ edge of a river

"The plane banked sharply to the left."
→ tilted

Word2Vec gives all three the same vector — a confused average of all three meanings.
Consequences
Semantic similarity breaks down for polysemous words.
cos(bank, river) is low even though it should be high in context 2.

Classification errors: a Sports article mentioning "the team batted well" and a Nature article about a "bat colony" will have very similar document vectors.
The fix — contextual embeddings
Instead of one vector per word, compute a vector that depends on the surrounding context.

bank in a financial sentence → one vector.
bank near river → a different vector.

This is what ELMo, BERT, and all modern Transformers do. Coming in Session 06.

Other limitations of static embeddings

Out-of-vocabulary (OOV)
Words not seen during training get no embedding.

"ChatGPT" in a GloVe model trained in 2014: unknown.
"SARS-CoV-2": unknown.
Any new product name, person, acronym: unknown.

Mean pooling assigns zero to OOV tokens — as if they were not there.
Bias
Embeddings absorb all statistical regularities of the training corpus, including human biases:

doctor − man + woman ≈ nurse
programmer − man + woman ≈ homemaker

The corpus reflects historical gender roles. The model learns them as facts. This is not just an NLP problem — it affects downstream decisions.
Mean pooling loses word order
"The dog bit the man" and "The man bit the dog" have the same bag of words.

They have the same mean-pooled embedding.

A mean-pooling classifier cannot distinguish them — same representation, same prediction. Word order is completely discarded.
Summary: Word2Vec and GloVe are powerful and still useful — but they are static, decontextualised, and biased. Session 05 (RNNs) will preserve word order. Session 06 (Transformers) will add context-dependence. The arc is: BoW → embeddings → sequences → attention.
